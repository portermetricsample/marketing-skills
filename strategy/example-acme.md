# Worked Example — "Acme" (fictional)

One case carried through all three layers of the stack, so each abstract framework has something concrete to point at. **Acme is invented — replace it entirely with your own.**

Acme turns raw support tickets into a live dashboard of what customers are struggling with, refreshed every hour.

> **This example is B2B SaaS — the frameworks are not.** If your business is B2C, local, or service-based, translate as you read: *proof* is reviews, ratings, before/after photos, or a track record (not "demos"); the *CTA* is "Book a visit" / "Get a quote" / "Call now" (not "Start free trial"); *pricing* may be a quote mechanism, not tiers; and the strategic narrative often leads on the **promised land** rather than a manufactured "change in the world." The steps below are identical for any business — only the surface vocabulary changes.

---

## Layer 1 — STP (`stp-marketing-strategy`)

**Segmentation (3 candidate segments, JTBD-first):**

| Segment | Job-to-be-done | Trigger | Alternative today |
|---|---|---|---|
| Support leads at mid-size SaaS | "Tell me what's breaking for customers before it becomes a churn wave" | A spike in tickets nobody caught until the QBR | Manual spreadsheet exports |
| Founders at pre-Series-A startups | "Read every ticket myself without reading every ticket" | Volume outgrew personal inboxing | Reading tickets one by one |
| Enterprise CX ops teams | "Feed categorized ticket data into our BI stack" | A board request for CSAT drivers | Data team + SQL |

**Targeting:** Concentrated on **support leads at 50–500-person SaaS**. Best fit (they feel the pain weekly, decide alone, no data team), reachable via support-community channels, underserved vs enterprise BI tools. The startup segment is too small to pay; enterprise wants an integration, not a product — both deprioritized.

**ICP with disqualifiers:** Head/Director of Support, 50–500 employees, lives in Zendesk or Intercom, already exports tickets by hand. *Disqualify:* fewer than ~10 tickets/day, or an in-house data team that would build this.

**Positioning statement:**
> For **heads of support at growing SaaS companies** who **need to catch customer problems before they become churn**, **Acme** is a **support analytics tool** that **turns every ticket into a live picture of what customers are struggling with**. Unlike **manual spreadsheet exports**, **Acme reads the ticket itself and refreshes hourly** — so a spike surfaces the day it starts, not next quarter.

Passes clarity, credibility (all provable), distinctiveness (name-swap fails — a helpdesk can't claim this), defensibility (categorization model isn't a weekend copy).

---

## Layer 2 — Messaging (`positioning-messaging`)

**Point of view:** Support volume now grows faster than teams can read it. Leaders who see what customers struggle with in real time keep them; leaders reading last quarter's tickets lose them to churn they never saw coming.

**Promised land:** Know what's hurting your customers today — not in the next review.

**Value themes:**

- **Hourly, not weekly** — Claim: catch a problem the day it spikes. Because: the dashboard refreshes every hour. Proof: customers flag issues 6 days earlier on average.
- **Reads the ticket, not just the tag** — Claim: miscategorized tickets still count. Because: categorization runs on what the customer wrote. Proof: recovers ~30% of tickets that were tagged wrong or not at all.
- **No analyst required** — Claim: live in an afternoon. Because: no SQL, sits on top of your helpdesk. Proof: median setup 3 hours, zero data-team involvement.

**Objections:** "Isn't this just my helpdesk's reporting?" → Helpdesk reports count tags; Acme reads the text, so it catches what tags miss. · "Another dashboard nobody opens?" → It pushes the one spike that matters to Slack, so you don't have to open it.

**Vocabulary:** use *tickets, spikes, churn signal, first-response time.* Avoid *NLP, taxonomy, ingestion pipeline.*

---

## Layer 3 — Landing page (`landing-page-copy`)

Traffic: category searches like "support ticket analytics" → **solution aware** → canonical solution-aware structure.

**Hero**
> **Headline:** See what your customers are struggling with — updated every hour
> **Subheader:** Acme reads every support ticket and turns it into a live dashboard, so you catch a problem the day it spikes, not in next quarter's review.
> **Bullets:** Categorizes by what customers write, not just the tag · Set up in an afternoon, no SQL · Works on top of Zendesk and Intercom
> **Primary CTA:** Start free trial *(14 days, no card)* · **Secondary:** See a live demo dashboard

**How the page renders the messaging:** each hero bullet maps to one value theme; the problem section mirrors the "spike nobody caught" trigger in the buyer's words; the three "how it works" steps stay under the 3-step cap; the FAQ answers the two objections verbatim. Every headline read top-to-bottom tells the whole story, and no adjective survives without evidence beside it.
